Before getting into the numbers, it helps to ask a simple question. At around US$311.63 per share, is JPMorgan Chase offering fair value, or are you taking on more price risk than you realize?
Over the past year the stock has returned 30.6%, with a 10.2% move over the last 30 days, a 1.7% decline over the last 7 days, and a 4.3% decline year to date, which can leave investors wondering whether recent strength is consolidating or starting to cool.
Recent news coverage has focused on JPMorgan Chase as one of the major US banks, with attention on how large institutions are positioned, how they are managing capital, and how they are exposed to key sectors of the economy. These themes often feed directly into market sentiment around the stock, which helps explain why short term price swings can be sharp even when the longer term story feels stable to many investors.
On Simply Wall St's valuation checks, JPMorgan Chase currently has a value score of 3 out of 6. The rest of this article will walk through what that means using different valuation approaches and then finish with a broader way to think about value that goes beyond any single model.

The Excess Returns model looks at how effectively a company turns its equity base into profits above its own cost of equity, then capitalizes those extra earnings into an estimated value per share.
For JPMorgan Chase, the starting point is a Book Value of US$128.38 per share and a Stable EPS of US$24.47 per share, based on weighted future Return on Equity estimates from 15 analysts. The average Return on Equity used in the model is 16.90%, while the Cost of Equity is set at US$11.51 per share. The difference between what the bank is modeled to earn on its equity and what that equity is assumed to cost produces an Excess Return of US$12.95 per share.
The model also uses a Stable Book Value of US$144.77 per share, based on weighted future Book Value estimates from 14 analysts, to extend these excess returns into the future and discount them back into today’s dollars. This process results in an Excess Returns intrinsic value estimate of US$429.89 per share, which implies the stock is 27.5% undervalued versus the current price of about US$311.63.

For a profitable bank like JPMorgan Chase, the P/E ratio is a useful shorthand because it links what you pay per share directly to the earnings that support that share price. It helps you gauge how much the market is currently willing to pay for each dollar of earnings.
What counts as a “normal” or “fair” P/E typically reflects how investors view a company’s growth prospects and risk profile. Higher expected earnings growth or lower perceived risk can justify a higher P/E, while slower growth or higher risk usually aligns with a lower P/E.
JPMorgan Chase currently trades on a P/E of 14.52x. That sits above the Banks industry average of 11.51x and also above the peer average of 13.08x. Simply Wall St’s Fair Ratio for JPMorgan Chase is 22.40x. This is a proprietary estimate of what the P/E might be given factors such as earnings growth, profit margins, industry, market cap and key risks. This tailored Fair Ratio can be more informative than a simple comparison against industry or peer averages because it adjusts for the company’s own characteristics.
Comparing the Fair Ratio of 22.40x with the current P/E of 14.52x suggests the shares are trading below that modeled level.

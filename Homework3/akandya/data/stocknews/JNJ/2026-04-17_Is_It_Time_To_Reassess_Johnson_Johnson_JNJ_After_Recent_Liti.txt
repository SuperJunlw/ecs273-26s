A Discounted Cash Flow, or DCF, model estimates what a company might be worth by projecting its future cash flows and discounting them back to today using a required rate of return. It is a way of translating long term cash generation into a single present value per share.
For Johnson & Johnson, the model used is a 2 Stage Free Cash Flow to Equity approach based on cash flow projections. The latest twelve month free cash flow stands at about $19.8b. Analysts provide explicit estimates out to several years, and Simply Wall St extrapolates beyond that, with projected free cash flow of $35.9b in 2030 and a series of annual figures in between. These future amounts are then discounted back to today to reflect risk and the time value of money.
On this basis, the DCF model points to an estimated intrinsic value of about $376.34 per share, compared with the current share price of $234.54. That implies the shares trade at a 37.7% discount to this intrinsic value, which indicates the stock appears undervalued on this metric.

For profitable businesses, the P/E ratio is a useful shorthand for what investors are currently willing to pay for each dollar of earnings. It helps you compare companies of different sizes using a single, earnings based yardstick.
What counts as a "normal" P/E usually reflects how the market views a company’s growth potential and risk. A higher P/E can reflect expectations of higher growth or lower perceived risk, while a lower P/E can reflect expectations of slower growth or higher risk.
Johnson & Johnson currently trades on a P/E of 26.85x. This is above the Pharmaceuticals industry average of 16.76x and also above the peer group average of 22.97x. On the surface, that indicates investors are paying a higher price for its earnings compared with many sector peers.
Simply Wall St’s Fair Ratio for Johnson & Johnson is 29.77x. This is a proprietary estimate of what the P/E might be given factors such as earnings growth, industry, profit margins, market cap and company specific risks. Because it blends these elements, it can be more tailored than a simple comparison with peers or the industry average.
With the current P/E of 26.85x below the Fair Ratio of 29.77x, the shares appear undervalued on this earnings based measure.

Investors may be wondering whether 3M is genuinely good value at its current price, or if the recent optimism has already been fully reflected in the share price.
The stock last closed at US$151.24, with returns of 1.4% over the past week, 0.9% over the past month, a 6.5% decline year to date, 18.1% over the past year, and 89.0% over three years.
Recent headlines have focused on 3M's restructuring efforts and ongoing legal settlements, which continue to shape how investors assess both risk and potential resilience. There has also been attention on portfolio simplification and cost initiatives, providing further context for recent share price moves.
3M currently has a valuation score of 4 out of 6. This invites a closer look at how different valuation methods view the stock and points to a potentially more helpful way to think about value that will be covered at the end of this article.

A Discounted Cash Flow, or DCF, model projects a company’s future cash flows and then discounts them back to today’s dollars to estimate what the business might be worth right now.
For 3M, the model used is a 2 Stage Free Cash Flow to Equity approach based on cash flow projections. The latest twelve month free cash flow is about $1.06b. Analyst estimates and extrapolated figures suggest free cash flow reaching about $5.16b in 2030, with a series of projected annual cash flows between 2026 and 2035. Each of these cash flows is discounted back to today using Simply Wall St’s methodology.
Bringing all these discounted cash flows together gives an estimated intrinsic value of about $212.80 per share. Compared with the recent share price of $151.24, the model points to an implied discount of 28.9%, which indicates that the stock screens as undervalued on this DCF view.

For profitable companies, the P/E ratio is a useful way to relate what you pay for a share to the earnings it currently generates. It also gives a quick sense of how the market is weighing growth expectations and risk, since higher growth or lower perceived risk often supports a higher “normal” P/E, while higher risk or weaker growth tends to justify a lower one.
3M currently trades on a P/E of 24.3x. That sits above the wider Industrials sector average of about 14.0x, but below the peer group average of 32.3x. Simply Wall St’s Fair Ratio framework estimates what a more tailored P/E might look like, based on factors such as earnings growth profile, profit margins, industry characteristics, market cap and company specific risks.
For 3M, the Fair Ratio is 30.9x, which is higher than the current 24.3x P/E. On this metric, the shares screen as trading below the level implied by those fundamentals oriented inputs.

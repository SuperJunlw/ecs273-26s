Wall Street's push into quantum computing is starting to look less like a near-term breakthrough story and more like a long-duration positioning bet, with early expectations being recalibrated across the industry. Roughly three years ago, Goldman Sachs (NYSE:GS) believed it had an edge after assembling a specialized research team and partnering with Amazon, but internal findings suggested that solving a key portfolio problem could require millions of years of computation and machines with at least 8 million logical qubits, far beyond today's systems with fewer than 100. That realization, alongside broader cost-cutting efforts, led to the dismantling of its quantum team. In contrast, JPMorgan Chase (NYSE:JPM) has continued to invest, building a team of more than 50 specialists focused on applications spanning optimization, machine learning, and cryptography, signaling a willingness to absorb near-term uncertainty in pursuit of a longer-term edge.

Across the industry, financial firms are still working to define where quantum computing could deliver tangible value, even as the technology remains largely in the research phase. JPMorgan has pointed to incremental progress, including work with Quantinuum's Helios processor to improve data processing efficiency and earlier collaboration with Amazon (NASDAQ:AMZN) on algorithms aimed at identifying uncorrelated assets to enhance portfolio diversification and risk management. Other institutions are also experimenting at varying levels, with UBS (NYSE:UBS) training around 50 quant analysts, BBVA exploring portfolio optimization, and Credit Agricole studying credit downgrade prediction. Meanwhile, HSBC (NYSE:HSBC) has reported as much as a 34% improvement in certain bond-trading forecasts using IBM hardware, though based on smaller datasets, underscoring both the promise and the limitations of current systems.
For now, the center of gravity remains with technology companies such as Google (NASDAQ:GOOG) and IBM (NYSE:IBM), along with firms like Rigetti Computing, which are building the hardware and software stack that financial institutions may eventually rely on. The gap between expectations and current capabilities has already shown up in market behavior, with quantum-related stocks posting sharp gains in 2024 before moderating as timelines extended and attention shifted back toward artificial intelligence. Industry participants have acknowledged that earlier projections may have been ahead of reality, and even proponents now frame progress in terms of years rather than immediate deployment. Still, banks appear to be maintaining selective engagement, suggesting that while near-term commercial impact could remain limited, the strategic incentive to stay involved in the development of quantum computing remains intact.

A Discounted Cash Flow, or DCF, model estimates what a business could be worth by projecting its future cash flows and discounting them back to today’s dollars.
For Exxon Mobil, the model starts with last twelve months free cash flow of about $27.8b. Analysts have provided explicit free cash flow estimates for the next few years, with projections such as $42.6b in 2026. Beyond the initial analyst horizon, Simply Wall St extrapolates cash flows further out, with projected free cash flow of $48.5b in 2030 and additional estimates through 2035, all expressed in dollars and discounted back using the same 2 Stage Free Cash Flow to Equity framework.
Adding these discounted cash flows together gives an estimated intrinsic value of about $295.25 per share. Compared with a current share price around $148, the DCF output indicates that, on this model, Exxon Mobil is trading at a lower price than the estimated intrinsic value.

For a mature, profitable company like Exxon Mobil, the P/E ratio is a useful way to relate what you pay for the stock to the earnings the business is currently generating. Investors generally accept that higher expected growth and lower risk can support a higher P/E, while slower growth or higher uncertainty usually point to a lower, more conservative range.
Exxon Mobil currently trades on a P/E of 21.35x. That sits above the Oil and Gas industry average of 14.90x, but below the peer group average of 31.03x. Simply Wall St also provides a proprietary “Fair Ratio” estimate of 30.57x, which reflects the P/E that might be expected after considering factors such as earnings growth, profit margins, industry, company size and identified risks.
This Fair Ratio can be more informative than a simple comparison with peers or the industry, because it adjusts for Exxon Mobil’s specific profile rather than assuming all companies in a sector deserve similar multiples. Lining up the Fair Ratio of 30.57x against the current 21.35x P/E suggests the shares are trading below that modelled range.
